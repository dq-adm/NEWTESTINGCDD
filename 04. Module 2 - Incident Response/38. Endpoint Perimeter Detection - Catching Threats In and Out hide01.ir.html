Endpoint Perimeter-level Detection

The endpoint perimeter is the second level or place where we can detect attacks. Technically speaking, an endpoint is any device connected to the network, such as desktops, laptops, and smartphones. We will use it here to refer to servers and end-user computers. 

But what does endpoint perimeter detection mean? Please think of the endpoint as a fort with a perimeter around it. Endpoint perimeter detection is the ability to spot any security breach or gap in the perimeter in both directions, in and out, like an external intruder trying to climb the perimeter to get in or an insider sneaking out to sell your secrets to the enemies. In computer security, endpoint perimeter detection is catching threats while entering or leaving the machine borders, typically through the network interface.

A real-world example of outbound endpoint-perimeter level detection is catching an attacker port scanning the internal network from a compromised machine. That represents an outbound threat to the scanning machine and an inbound threat to the scanned machine.

Now comes the important question, how can we detect attacks on the endpoint-perimeter level? There are not many available options, yet they are very effective. Available detection mechanisms are:

  • External scanning: running external scans across your endpoints to determine abnormal listening ports. A typical Windows corporate endpoint is expected to have some ports listening by default, such as 445 SMB and 135 RPC. However, ports like 4444 should be deemed suspicious. The best way to approach this is to baseline your network and whitelist standard listening ports, then flag anything else. Each organization has different technology solutions, and network requirements and common ports vary from one place to another.

    Many tools can help you here, such as vulnerability and port scanners. You can make this part of your periodic internal network vulnerability scan -using Nessus, for example, or script something to run mass port scans across your network using tools such as Nmap or MassScan.  One important thing to note is that your network and IT should be notified in advance before running these scans for multiple reasons:
    • The network team will have to configure the firewall to allow your scans, or it may be blocked by default. Proper network configuration will result in better visibility and ensure accurate scan results.
    • A couple of systems may stop responding during mass scans, which may result in some havoc if system admins are unaware of the scans you run.
    • Lastly, you must exclude the scanning machine IP address from your security device's detections. Otherwise, the scan will trigger a ton of false positive alerts.
       
  • Internal Watchers: using built-in and third-party endpoint tools to detect abnormal changes such as a new suspicious listening port or outbound connection. One significant limitation of external scans is that they provide a one-time snapshot, meaning anything that happens after the current scan ends and before the next scan starts will not be detected, leaving behind a big blind area. The key takeaway is that regardless of the attack vector, the attacker needs to communicate with the compromised machine in one way or another. Detecting attacks at the endpoint perimeter level is an efficient way to catch threats that bypass your network perimeter detection. Both external scanning and internal watchers complement each other and are used together to provide comprehensive detection.
