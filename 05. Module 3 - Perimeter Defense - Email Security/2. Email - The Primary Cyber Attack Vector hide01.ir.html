Perimeter Defense - Email Security > Email: The Primary Cyber Attack Vector


CyberDefense has evolved recently and become a critical aspect of any business. As a result, many resources, theories, and guidelines have explained the best way to secure enterprise networks.

As defenders, we found ourselves in one of two situations, building a security program from scratch or contributing to an existing one. That leaves us with a popular question: Where should we start -from a technical perspective? The logical answer is; to start with what has the most significant impact on the overall security posture and requires minimal effort; The most widely used attack vector...email system! Like firewalls, the email system is the first line of defense against cyber attacks.


Many published studies suggest that email is the most widely used attack vector.

  • "91% of targeted attacks involve spear-phishing emails, reinforcing the belief that spear phishing is a primary means by which APT attackers infiltrate target networks" - Phishing Email Most Favored Attack by TrendMicro.
  • "The Initial Compromise represents the methods intruders use to first penetrate a target organizations network. As with most other APT groups, spear-phishing is APT1s most commonly used technique. The spear-phishing emails contain either a malicious attachment or a hyperlink to a malicious file."  - APT1: Attack Lifecycle by Mandiant.
  • "95% of all state-affiliated espionage attacks relied on phishing in some way" - Data Breach Report by Verizon.
  • "Email is still the most commonly used attack vector for both opportunistic and targeted attacks." - Market Guide for Email Security by Gartner.
  • "Phishing is still the number one attack vector in use today"  X-Force Threat Intelligence Index 2020 by IBM.


There are multiple reasons why Email attacks are so popular and effective today:

  1. Ease of use. Everybody knows how to use it!
  2. Popularity. Every organization has it.
  3. It targets the weakest link in the security chain. Most end-users lack security awareness basics. Even security professionals sometimes fall for phishing attacks.
  4. Rewarding. Attackers can target thousands of users with a single email. If it does not work for one user, it can work for another.
  5. Highly depictive and almost impossible to block. Attackers can use multiple domains during an attack to disguise protection. If one domain gets blocked, a new one can be instantly registered and utilized. Attackers also have the flexibility of blending multiple techniques in a single attack, such as social engineering, malware, exploits, and identity spoofing.
  6. Cheap. Sending thousands of emails costs a few dollars, whether through hiring bots or deploying disposable email servers.
  7. Many organizations have fragile email security. Security teams face some difficulties hardening incoming and outgoing email servers because of the criticality of the email function to the business. A single mistake during the hardening process that interrupts the service may lead to havoc and blame for security teams.
  8. Highly Reusable. Attackers can use the same technique multiple times in a single attack without being caught. For example, after the attacker takes over a regular user account, he can use it to internally phish other privileged users such as network/system admins and VIP users.

That made email the number one choice for attackers among different attack vehicles.
