Perimeter Defense - Email Security > [T4]: Secure Accounts with Multi-Factor Authentication


Multi-factor authentication provides an extra layer of security for users' accounts. It's designed to ensure that the account owner is the only person who can access the account, even if the attacker knows the password. It works by requesting two pieces of information to access the account; the password and something else the account owner physically has access to. It could be a one-time code generated from the user's mobile, a hardware token, or a push notification sent to the account owner's mobile device.


Regardless of how robust your mail security system is, there is a possibility for users' accounts to be compromised. MFA is the most effective preventive control against accounts takeover I am aware of. If I have been asked to select a single control to protect user accounts, it will be MFA. However, do not let that give you a false sense of immunity. Attackers come up with new techniques every day to bypass MFA protection. For example, the phishing page can prompt users to enter their one-time code after submitting their credentials. Then, the attacker instantly uses the submitted username+password+one time code before the one-time code expires. Using SMS and mobile-generated one-time code is not considered a safe practice nowadays. Whenever possible, use push notifications and hardware tokens to authenticate your users.


Attackers may try to bypass MFA by using legacy email protocols that do not support MFA, such as IMAP, MAPI, and POP. So, it's crucial to ensure these protocols are blocked if there is no business need. Email accounts used by corporate printers are usually top on the target list for attackers because they are traditionally excluded from MFA and password expiration policies.


MFA is not limited only to business accounts; almost all service providers such as Google, Microsoft, Amazon, and Apple offer multi-factor authentication for their services.

 

MFA deployment may face some resistance from business and IT colleagues. Thus, its essential to understand your business needs and ensure that deploying MFA is a business enabler rather than a show stopper. Its more of a selling/marketing effort rather than a technical exercise.
