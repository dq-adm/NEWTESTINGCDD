Perimeter Defense - Email Security > [T4]: User Education: Key to Email Security

 

User awareness

Its pretty rare nowadays to see a cyberattack that does not involve targeting the human element. Most attacks include dealing with end-users in one way or another, such as phishing and social engineering. Organizations are good at spending money on technical solutions to secure their networks. Still, little if anything into non-technical solutions that protect from threats where technical controls are ineffective. Email security is no different; while there are many technological solutions, non-technical solutions are critical for comprehensive protection. Simply put, we cannot solve all security problems with technology.

 

Almost all email attacks involve actions performed by end-users, excluding some RCE attacks. So, its imperative to spend a good deal of resources on educating end-users -or, as I call them, pending targets- on risks they should avoid and the steps they should take -if they are unsure about what to do in specific scenarios.

 

Think of it for a moment; irrespective of how advanced or stealth a malware delivered via an email could be, nothing will happen if the user does not click/execute it. Training employees to recognize email attacks helps to reduce the probability that they will click on a malicious link or open an attachment.

"When it comes to email security, nothing beats an educated user."

Many training providers offer security awareness services to help organizations educate their users about cybersecurity. In the following section, we will list the most popular awareness tips for email security.

 

  • Avoid being a target. Educate your users on the importance of solely using their corporate email addresses for business use. Remember, an email attack cannot start without a target email.
  • Link mismatch: Users should be able to recognize the final destination of a hyperlink. Attackers use shortening services or tricky links (https://google.com/) to hide the final destination of a hyperlink. Instructing users to manually copy and paste links into their browsers or hovering -without clicking- over links gives them a chance to find any discrepancy.

    For shortened links, many online services -such as https://unshorten.it offer a way to reveal shortened URL's final destination without visiting it. Its also possible to append + at the end of goo.gl and bit.ly shortened URLs to display the link's true destination.
  • Reply-to trick: The Reply-To header is added when the email sender wants any replies to the email to go to that particular email address rather than the one in the "From" address. That usually shows up as a separate field in the email client. Microsoft states that no technique (SPF, DKIM, DMARC, or any other technology) protects the Reply-To header.  Attackers use this in conjunction with spoofing to redirect the victim's reply - often containing sensitive information the attacker requested in the first mail- to their inbox rather than the inbox of the spoofed sender. Instruct users to eye-verify emails when replying to messages in the "To" field.
  • Psychology manipulation: Attackers use an extreme sense of urgency to make their targets take the desired actions. Instruct users to flag messages that ask for immediate actions (such as 'click here or you will lose access to your inbox'). Attackers are excellent at using curiosity and emotions to trick users into opening links and attachments.
  • Request for sensitive information: Under normal circumstances, one should not receive legitimate requests to send sensitive information via email. Instruct users to flag and report -to the security team- any message that asks for sensitive information such as passwords, confidential documents, or credit card information.
  • Fake Unsubscribe: Attackers send spam emails with a fake unsubscribe link -usually a malicious link- at the end of the message to trick users into opening the link. In some advanced attacks, attackers may use this step to identify active targets with a higher probability of responding to phishing emails. Then, the attacker craft a personalized email to each identified target, increasing the odds of falling for the attack.
  • No Ultimate Trust: Users should be aware that attacks do not come from unknown senders only. Attackers use compromised accounts to attack more users. Users should not assume that an email is safe because it was sent from someone they know.
  • Bypass it All: Recently, We have seen an attack trend targeting corporate users via their personal email addresses. Attackers are aware of the fact of how hard it is to bypass corporate email security controls. Thus, they started to reach out to corporate users over their personal inboxes to phish them or compromise their machines. Social media made discovering the personal email of business users very easy. For example, attackers use a LinkedIn feature to find corporate users' personal emails. Instruct users to keep their digital footprint minimal and take their online privacy seriously.
