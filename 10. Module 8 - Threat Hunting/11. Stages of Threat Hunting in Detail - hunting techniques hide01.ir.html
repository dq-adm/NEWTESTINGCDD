Stage 2: what are the available hunting techniques? 

The second stage is where the actual hunting happens. At the abstract level, hunting is nothing but looking for signs of attack. There are many different threat-hunting techniques that analysts can use to identify potential threats:

  • Searching:

    The simplest and most common threat-hunting technique. It involves using search tools to locate specific indicators of compromise (IOCs) within an organization's data sources. For example, exploit kits utilize HTTP redirects to infect the target. Querying the weblogs for response code 302 may spot exploit kits.

     

  • Clustering:

    A technique used to group together related data points or events to identify patterns or anomalies that may indicate a potential threat. This may involve using visualization tools to plot the data points and look for clusters or patterns that stand out from the rest. This resource explains how Cisco Talos utilize clustering for hunting threats in large datasets.

     

  • Stack counting:


    A technique used to track the occurrence of specific indicators of compromise (IOCs) over time. This involves counting the number of times a particular IOC appears and analyzing any values that stand out as particularly high or low compared to the rest. For example, if Zeek is deployed in your network, you can list all SSL certificate issuers seen across your network. By stacking the number of occurrences of each issuer, we can highlight abnormal ones.
