Threat Hunting > [T1]: Proactive Human-driven Threat Hunting: Automation

5-Can be partially automated but not fully

Threat hunting is mainly a manual exercise and can be partially automated using specialized tools and techniques, such as machine learning and artificial intelligence. However, it cannot be fully automated because it requires the input and expertise of a human analyst to interpret the results of any automated processes and to make decisions about how to respond to potential threats. 

For example, as an analyst, you may be aware that a specific employee is on leave. Hunting for authentication requests initiated from his account should flag suspicious behavior, which is expected in another context (if he was not on vacation). Such tiny details and context awareness make threat hunting very effective in catching attacks flying below the radar level.

You can automate specific tasks within a hunt, such as querying logs and parsing results. However, you cannot automate everything because sometimes, you will not have the capability to feed all types of intel you receive as a human analyst into an automated tool.

 

That human element is what makes hunting very effective in catching undetected attacks. As the "SANS 2018 Threat Hunting Survey Results" says, ThreatHunting begins where automation ends, although it heavily leverages automation.

 

The following table summarizes the difference between traditional detection and threat hunting.

Traditional Detection Threat Hunting
Signature-based. Also known as Indicators of Compromise (IOCs) (e.g., hash, signature, malicious domain or IP, etc). In other words, things that can be put into a signature and alerted for. Behavior-based. Works with tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) (e.g., how an attack is performed regardless of its fingerprints). In other words, things that cannot be put into a signature.
Reactive Proactive
Evidence-based investigation: starts with confirmed evidence. Hypothesis-based investigation: starts with an assumption.
Tool-driven: the tool detects and alerts. Human-driven: the analyst detects and alerts.
It can be fully automated. It can be partially automated.

 

Effective threat hunting requires a combination of technical expertise and analytical skills. It requires a deep understanding of how an organization's systems and networks operate and the ability to identify anomalies (deviation from the baseline) that may indicate the presence of a threat. Threat hunters must also be able to work collaboratively with other security team members and have strong communication skills to share findings and recommendations effectively.
