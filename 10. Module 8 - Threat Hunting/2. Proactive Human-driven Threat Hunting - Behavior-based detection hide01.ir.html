Threat Hunting > [T1]: Proactive Human-driven Threat Hunting: Behavior-based detection

1-Behavior-based detection:

Threat Hunting focuses on detecting attack behavior, also known as tactics, techniques, and procedures TTPs (e.g., how the attack is performed rather than its fingerprints). Consider a typical day in a SOC where your threat intelligence feeds report the discovery of a new attack group that has been observed in the wild. This group is known to use a tool called Mimikatz to steal credentials.

Traditional signature-based detection involves deploying an alert for the attack fingerprints, also known as Indicators of compromise IOCs (e.g., hash, signature, malicious domain or IP.etc). Alerting for any executable on the network that matches the hash (IOC) of Mimikatz is a traditional signature-based detection. The system will reactively alert if a match is found based on the evidence it has discovered.

Threat hunting, on the other hand, would focus on understanding how Mimikatz works and attempting to detect the techniques (TTPs) that it uses. For example, to detect credential dumping, threat hunters might search for any high-privileged child processes that low-privileged parent processes have spawned. A list of credential-dumping techniques can be found here.

 

David Biancos pyramid of pain

 

David Bianco created the above diagram to show how difficult it is to perform detection using different types of artifacts. At the bottom, you will find stuff that is trivial to detect and, at the same time, trivial for the attacker to replace or manipulate. For example, the hash of a malicious executable can be easily detected using traditional automated tools. And at the same time, trivial for the attacker to obfuscate the executable, change its hash, and still perform the same functionality.

On the top, you will find TTPs, which are hard to detect because security tools are not well-suited to ingest and, at the same time, challenging for the attacker to replace or manipulate. Following the same example, it's hard to detect a malicious executable based on how it works (and not its fingerprints), and at the same time, it's costly for the attacker to develop a new malware or change how it works.

Think of IOCs Vs. TTP as signature detection versus behavior detection. Hunting can occur at any of the above levels. However, it primarily focuses on stuff that is challenging for traditional detection and, at the same time, sticky to the attacker (top of the pyramid). In other words, behavior-based detection.
