Security Operation Center (SOC)  - Overview

The first module will discuss security operations center (SOC) fundamentals. The place you, as a defender, will spend most of your time. Let's start by explaining some basics.

 

Information Security and Cyber Security

Information Security and Cyber Security are popular terms you may come across in the field. Sometimes they are used interchangeably to refer to the same thing. However, there is a slight difference. Cybersecurity is about protecting digital data in cyberspace, while information security, also known as InfoSec, is about protecting information in cyberspace and beyond, digital or non-digital. In other words, Cyber Security is a subset of Information Security. 

Nowadays, information security is a vital necessity in every modern organization. Unfortunately, many organizations have mistaken perspectives on information security. Some may think that using technical tools will save them from any threat. In reality, adequate Cyber security requires more than tools and patches. It requires a clear understanding of the threat landscape and business risks. In the end, the ultimate goal of the cyber security unit is to support the business.

Security is critical for enterprises and organizations of all sizes and industries. Information security is a set of processes, tools, and policies. The core pillars of information security are confidentiality, integrity, and availability. These principles institute what we call the CIA triad.

 

Confidentiality

Asserts that all the information and data are accessible only by persons who are authorized to have access. It is essential to ensure unauthorized parties do not have access to the data. Theft of Personal Identifiable Information (PII) is an example of a confidentiality attack.

 

Integrity

Integrity aims to protect information against unauthorized modifications; in other words, the trustworthiness of data. Data must be consistent, accurate, and trustworthy throughout the information lifecycle, at rest (on disk), in transit (on the wire), and during processing. Think of the consequences of a bank customer who can manipulate/hack his online banking account to increase his account balance.

 

Availability

Availability seeks to ensure the information is available to authorized users when needed. Denial of Service (DoS) is an example of an availability attack. On Feb. 28, 2018, GitHub, a platform for software developers, was hit with a DDoS attack that clocked in at 1.35 terabits per second and lasted for roughly 20 minutes. According to GitHub, the traffic was traced back to "over a thousand different autonomous systems (ASNs) across tens of thousands of unique endpoints."

Least Privilege and Need To Know

The principle of least privilege is a concept in which a user is given the minimum level of access or permissions needed to perform the job. For example, the users should not be granted READ and WRITE access if they only need to read the data.

Need To Know means the user must have a legitimate reason to access data. In other words, there should be a valid justification for granting data access. For example, If only one HR user is responsible for the payroll system, then other HR users should not be given access to the payroll system. Another example is when external auditors come and audit your network, they should receive adequate information to perform their job on a need-to-know basis.

 

Defense in Depth

 

Defense in Depth Example - zoom in

Defense in Depth (DID) means layering a series of defensive controls to protect an asset or data. If one control fails, another steps up immediately to stop the attack. This multi-layered approach increases the overall security of a system and protects against many different attack vectors.

 

Information Security Management Program

The Information Security Management program's main aim is to ensure that the business operates at acceptable risk levels. In other words, making sure the organization will not experience a risk from a cyber attack that it cannot tolerate. The Information Security Management System (ISMS) developed by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO), referred to as ISO 27001, is an example of a business-driven framework (policies, procedures, standards, and guidelines) to help information security professionals establish a good level of security.
