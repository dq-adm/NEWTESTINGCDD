Growth, Skill Development, and Training Plans

"Anyone who stops learning is old, whether at twenty or eighty. Anyone who keeps learning stays young. The greatest thing in life is to keep your mind young." - Henry Ford 

The first step in setting up your training plans is to ensure that roles and levels are clearly defined. You can draft a plan if you need to learn about the roles your SOC need and the levels of expertise. Let's take the DFIR role as an example. DFIR stands for digital forensics and incident response and can involve collecting, preserving, and analyzing forensic evidence to paint a complete, detailed picture of incidents. 

You can see three primary DFIR skills: collection, preserving, and analysis. Going one step further, we need to identify training resources relevant to each skill. Training resources are not limited to certifications and instructor-led training. In fact, they involve any action that contributes to leveling up the targeted skill, such as practical exercises, competitions, CTFs, and simulations. 

At CyberDefenders, we have a community initiative, BlueYard, focused on Digital Forensics and Incident Response (DFIR). This initiative creates an environment where anyone, regardless of expertise level, can investigate intrusions and enhance their DFIR skills.

Not only that, but you can think of non-traditional ways to improve your technical skills, such as teaching! Do you know that teaching a specific topic is the best way to understand it? This comes in various formats, such as contributing to the community via Security BSides talks or presenting at a security conference like BlackHat and Defcon.

Another critical thing to consider is specialization. In the early days, a security engineer was expected to take care of anything related to security, whether it was an investigation, forensic analysis, or engineering task. That is not the situation anymore! Businesses started to realize the importance of cyber security and are willing to invest more money in improving the organization's security. In some organizations, the headcount of security teams may exceed 30 individuals. 

It's understood that there are some basics that every SOC team member should be aware of, such as networking, scripting, and operating systems. But, at a specific stage, team members must have a skill development roadmap suitable for their skills. 

Aside from technical skills, soft skills are fundamental and can dramatically impact achieving the security program goals. Communication skills come first at the top of the queue. Being a good communicator will decrease detection time, minimize breach damage, and increase user satisfaction and vice versa. Remember that, in every investigation, you are not working alone and will have to deal with many stakeholders, such as end-users, customers, and IT/Network administrators. From our humble experience, user interviews, if done correctly, can save security teams considerable time. Being a good communicator will allow you to determine what questions to ask, in which order, and how to articulate them. 

Another important soft skill is staying calm and controlling the adrenaline rush during breaches. Remaining calm will prevent you from coming to the wrong conclusions. Remember, during investigations, early assumptions are often proven wrong. 

Presentation skills are very important too. You will present information to other individuals at any stage of your career. For example, if you are a SOC analyst, you will present the findings to the SOC lead and other colleagues. A SOC manager will present to the security director. A director will present to C-level executives and so on. Educating users on security and increasing their awareness levels requires decent presentation skills.

Having an internal wiki and sharing knowledge among team members is another great way to impove team skills. Have a weekly meeting for your team and let one team member give a technical talk about a topic they master. That will ensure smooth knowledge transfer between team members.
